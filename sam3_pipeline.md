# Robust Lecture Slide Extraction Pipeline using SAM 3.1

It is always a smart move to stress-test an architecture before writing a single line of code. Here is a battle-tested procedure optimized for speed, reliability, and clean outputs, using Meta's SAM 3.1.

## Phase 1: Smart Sampling & Detection

**1. Downsample the Video (Temporal FPS Drop)**
Instead of processing 30 frames per second, extract one frame per second (1 fps). Lecture slides rarely appear and disappear in less than a second. This cuts your processing time by 96%.

**2. SAM 3.1 Zero-Shot Detection**
Pass the 1 fps keyframes to SAM 3.1. 
* Use the positive prompt: `"projector screen, presentation slide"`.
* Use the negative prompt: `"person, human, hand, shadow"`. (This forces SAM to ignore the professor if they step in front of the screen).

**3. Presence Filtering**
Check SAM’s Presence Score. If the score is `< 0.75` (e.g., the camera panned to the audience or the projector turned off), discard the frame and log the slide as "Absent."

---

## Phase 2: Geometric Refinement & Deskewing

**4. Contour Approximation (The Polygon Fix)**
SAM 3.1 outputs a pixel mask, not a perfect polygon. If the professor’s head overlaps the corner of the slide, the mask will bend around them. Take the binary mask generated by SAM and use an algorithm like **Douglas-Peucker** (available in OpenCV as `cv2.approxPolyDP`). This forces the organic, pixelated mask into a strict mathematical polygon. 
* *Crucial Logic Check:* If the resulting polygon does not have exactly 4 corners (because of heavy occlusion), **do not extract the slide from this frame**. Skip to the next second until you get a clean, 4-corner view.

**5. Homography Transform**
Once you have exactly 4 corners, use OpenCV’s `cv2.warpPerspective` to flatten the slide. Map those 4 coordinates to a standard, high-resolution rectangle (e.g., 1920x1080). 

---

## Phase 3: Content Tracking & Transition Detection

**6. Perceptual Hashing or SSIM**
Now that you have a perfectly flat, clean image of the slide, you can efficiently check for content changes. Compare the current deskewed slide to the previously saved slide using **SSIM (Structural Similarity Index)** or a simple **Perceptual Hash (pHash)**. 
* These algorithms are extremely lightweight and specifically designed to detect if the visual structure (text, graphs) has changed, ignoring minor lighting shifts.

**7. Triggering the "Change"**
If the SSIM drops below a specific threshold (e.g., `< 0.85`), a slide transition has occurred. 

---

## Phase 4: Timestamping & Output

**8. Log and Aggregate**
* When a new slide is detected via SSIM, log the `start_time`.
* Keep overwriting the "best image" for that slide while it is on screen (e.g., keeping the frame with the highest SAM presence score and sharpest focus).
* When SSIM detects another change, log the `end_time` for the previous slide and save the final, flattened image to your disk alongside the timestamps.
